How you can develop the technique of reflective listening?

  • The key is to create space for you to listen and the other person to speak!
  • This requires that you turn your attention out-wards.
  • One way for you to start practising reflective listening would be to simply notice and listen to the sounds around you (and this is not necessarily speech) to hear what you been missing.
  • Devote a specific period of your day, about 10 minutes, to deliberately sit down in a place where a variety of sounds are likely to naturally occur such as a park, practise absorbing the sounds, be curious by trying to identify all the different origins of the sounds around you, a bird singing nearby, the rustle of a breeze through the leaves of a tree, a plane flying overhead and notice the qualities that make each sound unique.
  • Clearly there are situations such as being in built up areas in cities which will present you with a greater challenge for reflective listening. Solutions include using a CD /DVD player for a piece of music where you have to identify all the different instruments and voices playing, an audio book where you listen carefully to all the changes in: intonation, style, accent and speed of the person reading.
  • Another approach is to practise this approach in everyday conversations to actively listen to someone speaking, to reflect back to them what they have just said to you, to ask for confirmation from them how accurately you have reflected back what they were saying to you.
  • You can formally practise reflective listening with another person by setting up this exercise:
    1. The listener for a particular period of time – say 3 minutes - restricts him/herself to only asking questions. Then at the end he/she provides a short summary back.
    2. The listener for a particular period of time – say 3 minutes - restricts him/herself to only asking questions. Then at the end he/she provides a short summary back.
    3. The speaker comments then on the accuracy of the summary. If the summary was not accurate the process continues until such time as the speaker feels the listener has accurately understood what was being said.
    4. Then reverse the roles.
  • You can improve your listening capability by deliberately using as part of your conversational style more open-ended questions such as:
    1. “What do you mean by that?”
    2. “Would you explain that to me, please?”
    3. “Can I clarify that you mean.”
  • Such questions offer a structured way to ensure you listen more.
  • When your reflective listening skills are improved notice how
    1. your understanding of what has been said increases,
    2. it becomes easier for you to know what to say in reply,
    3. you appear to get on better with other people
